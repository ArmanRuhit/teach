# Worked Examples — the theory the engine runs on

Why the teach loop is shaped the way it is, grounded in Cognitive Load Theory and the Chen et al. step-level study. Read this when deciding *how much* to demonstrate, *which* step to work hardest, and *when* to stop demonstrating.

## The core mechanism

A worked example shows a problem **and its full solution**, step by step, instead of asking the learner to solve from scratch. Novices gain because they **borrow** a ready-made solution schema instead of **randomly searching** for one. Random search overloads working memory (very limited capacity); the worked example frees that capacity to understand and store the method. This is why the unit loop teaches *before* it challenges.

## When to give a full worked example

- **Novice on this specific material.** The benefit comes from the learner lacking prior schemas. (Check prior teachings first — a known unit is no longer novice ground.)
- **High element interactivity** — many pieces that must be held in mind at once (solving `3x + 5 = 8` forces juggling 3, x, +, 5, =, 8 together). This is where a full demonstration earns its cost.
- **Skip it for low-interactivity material** — isolated facts, one-line lookups. Worked examples add little here; state it and move to the challenge.
- **Stop giving it as expertise grows** — the **expertise-reversal effect**: what was high-interactivity for a novice becomes one chunk for an expert, and a full example becomes redundant guidance that *adds* load. This is the rationale behind the fade controller, not just economy.

## Designing one worked example

1. **Pick a representative problem** that exercises the target schema — not an edge case.
2. **Break the solution into complete, explicit steps.** Each step = one meaningful transformation, shown fully. Don't skip the "obvious" intermediate move; obvious-to-you is the high-load step for a novice.
3. **Show the reasoning where the *why* matters**, so the learner can self-explain.
4. **Keep explanation next to its referent** — no split attention (diagram on one screen, its description on another).
5. **Strip extraneous detail** — decorative context, redundant restatement, and noisy formatting all consume the working memory that should go to the method.

## Which step to work hardest

The worked-example benefit is **not** always largest on Step 1. In Chen et al. it was strongest on the **middle step** — the one that links backward (to Step 1) and forward (to Step 3), carrying extra *relational* load during learning even with fewer raw elements. **Find the connective step and give it the clearest treatment.** Don't reflexively front-load support onto the first step.

## Sequencing across the lesson (the fade ladder)

- **Pair each example with immediate practice** — worked example → similar problem to solve. Studying then applying beats studying two examples or solving two problems; it forces the learner to actually process the example.
- **Fade as steps and units proceed.** Early/high-load steps stay fully worked; later low-load steps become **completion problems** — first steps given, learner finishes the rest. Eventually drop to independent solving.
- **Order: worked → completion → independent**, mirroring the learner's growing schemas.

## Reading whether it's working

- **Per-step effort (Paas 7-point):** "How much mental effort did that step take?" (1 = very little, 7 = very much). A cheap live signal — high means keep it worked, consistently low means fade.
- **Felt challenge** tracks load closely ("this is a challenging step for me," 1–7).
- **Outcomes:** retention (problems like those taught) and transfer (structurally different problems). Worked examples help both, but transfer gains are smaller — transfer needs more practice and automation than one short lesson provides, which is why challenges stay *near*-transfer.

## Checklist

- [ ] Learner is a novice on this specific material
- [ ] Material is genuinely high in element interactivity
- [ ] Solution broken into complete, explicit steps
- [ ] Reasoning shown where the *why* matters
- [ ] Clearest treatment given to the connective (often middle) step
- [ ] No split attention; explanation sits next to its referent
- [ ] Extraneous detail removed
- [ ] Each example followed by a matching problem to solve
- [ ] Guidance fades over steps / over the lesson (completion problems)
- [ ] Plan to drop examples once schemas form (expertise reversal)
- [ ] (Optional) per-step effort rating to verify load reduction
